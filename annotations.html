Ecological Embeddedness, Living Systems, and Regenerative Organizing

This section situates organizations within living systems rather than apart from them. Drawing on systems thinking, ecological science, and regenerative theory, these texts challenge the extractive logic embedded in conventional organizational worldviews and offer frameworks for understanding organizational purpose as participation in rather than extraction from living worlds.

36
36  ·  Ecological
Whiteman, G., & Cooper, W. H. (2000). Ecological embeddedness. Academy of Management Journal, 43(6), 1265–1282. https://doi.org/10.2307/1556349
This study examines how indigenous Cree tallymen in northern Canada manage land through what the authors call ecological embeddedness — a deep, experiential attunement to the natural environment built through direct presence, cultural belief, and intergenerational knowledge. Using an 18-month ethnographic study, Whiteman and Cooper argue that ecological embeddedness is not a metaphor but a materially enacted relationship: the tallymen do not just observe the environment, they are constituted by it and through it. The study traces four dimensions of ecological embeddedness: physical exposure to land, ecological knowledge grounded in place, ecological actions oriented toward sustainability, and cultural beliefs about human–land interdependence. These dimensions operate together to produce a mode of environmental management that is fundamentally incompatible with the extractive rationality of conventional corporate management.

This text is important because it brings forth the principle of ecological embeddedness and its contribution to sustainability in management contexts. Research is needed on how corporate executives can best implement indigenous approaches. For example, how much exposure to nature is needed — and what kinds — to shift worldview and decision-making toward ecological awareness? The study challenges the prevailing extractive logic of corporate management by showing what leadership looks like when it is genuinely embedded in and accountable to living systems. For this literature review, Whiteman and Cooper provide an empirical anchor for Section 7's ecological and living-systems orientation: they show that ecological worldview is not abstract philosophy but enacted, embodied practice.

37
37  ·  Ecological
Capra, F. (1996). The web of life: A new scientific understanding of living systems. Anchor Books.
Capra offers a systemic understanding of living systems which can be very useful for business leaders, decision makers, sustainability professionals, and educators — providing a contrast to paradigms of mechanism and offering a different viewpoint. His work discusses social systems, ecosystems, and the deep patterns of life across scales, drawing on systems biology, quantum physics, and complexity theory to argue that life is a network phenomenon that cannot be understood through reductionist analysis alone.

Capra suggests that solutions to our current ecological and social crises require a radical shift in our perception, worldview, values, and thinking. He speaks of deep ecological awareness, social ecology, and ecofeminism as related and important schools of thought that support regenerative organizations. He outlines a progression from rational, linear, and reductionist thinking toward synthesis, holistic, and nonlinear thinking, and from mechanistic to relational. Values must shift from competition to cooperation, from expansion to conservation, from quantity to quality, and from domination to partnership and co-creation. Systems thinking, which emerges from organismic biologists who rejected mechanism, holds that relationships rather than parts help you understand a system. A system cannot be understood through analysis alone.

For this literature review, Capra provides foundational grounding for the ecological worldview that Section 7 develops. His synthesis of living systems thinking offers a robust philosophical and scientific basis for regenerative organizing — showing that the shift in organizational worldview required by this review's research question is not arbitrary but reflects deep patterns in how life itself is organized.
