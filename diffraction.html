Research question DependabilityCredibilityConfirmability
How can embodied gardening practices generate a form of thinking-being that supports ecocentric, relational ethics in sustainable organising?
How does ecological embeddedness manifest in Cree land management practice, and what does this reveal about the limits of conventional management sustainability frameworks?
Qualitative approach justified DependabilityCredibilityConfirmability
Posthumanist methodology requires embodied presence and participation; qualitative approach enables exploration of relational, affective knowing that quantitative methods cannot access.
Ethnographic methods enable access to the lived, place-based practices of ecological embeddedness that surveys or structured interviews alone cannot capture.
Epistemological / ontological positioning Confirmability
Posthumanist relational ontology; challenges anthropocentric epistemology; moves away from the human as reference point; ecocentric — knowledge is produced through embodied, more-than-human entanglement.
Interpretive / constructivist; draws on Indigenous ecological knowledge systems and ecological science; knowledge understood as situated in specific places and practices.
Methodology / design DependabilityCredibilityConfirmability
Qualitative; garden as vignette and empirical site; thinking-being as method — a form of reflexive, embodied philosophical inquiry at the intersection of practice and theory.
Ethnography; extended participant observation with Cree hunters and tallymen in subarctic Canada; fieldwork as primary mode of inquiry.
Context
Setting / field site CredibilityTransferability
A garden — simultaneously a material site and conceptual figure. Location not specified; the garden functions as a universalised yet particular space of encounter.
James Bay subarctic, Canada. Cree hunting territory and seasonal land-use areas. Local scale, bounded by Indigenous territorial land management.
Case rationale DependabilityCredibilityConfirmability
The garden is chosen for its capacity to enact thinking-being — it is neither purely natural nor cultural, offering a site for relational posthuman ethics to unfold.
Cree hunters are selected as exemplary cases of ecological embeddedness — their practice enacts the very concept the paper seeks to theorise. Purposive case selection.
