Research Design
Research question
How can embodied gardening practices generate a form of thinking-being that supports ecocentric, relational ethics in sustainable organising?
How does ecological embeddedness manifest in Cree land management practice, and what does this reveal about the limits of conventional management sustainability frameworks?
Qualitative approach justified
Posthumanist methodology requires embodied presence and participation; qualitative approach enables exploration of relational, affective knowing that quantitative methods cannot access.
Ethnographic methods enable access to the lived, place-based practices of ecological embeddedness that surveys or structured interviews alone cannot capture.
Epistemological / ontological positioning
Posthumanist relational ontology; challenges anthropocentric epistemology; moves away from the human as reference point; ecocentric — knowledge is produced through embodied, more-than-human entanglement.
Interpretive / constructivist; draws on Indigenous ecological knowledge systems and ecological science; knowledge understood as situated in specific places and practices.
Methodology / design
Qualitative; garden as vignette and empirical site; thinking-being as method — a form of reflexive, embodied philosophical inquiry at the intersection of practice and theory.
Ethnography; extended participant observation with Cree hunters and tallymen in subarctic Canada; fieldwork as primary mode of inquiry.
Data Collection
Site & participants
Gardens as ecological sites; plants, soil, seasons, and human gardeners as co-participants; non-human others as central to the inquiry.
James Bay region, subarctic Canada; Cree hunters and tallymen (land managers); ecological community including land, species, and seasonal cycles as implicit participants.
Sampling strategy
Purposive — Cree community selected for deep ecological embeddedness; specific tallymen as key informants with intensive land knowledge.
Data collection methods
Embodied engagement with gardens; reflexive inquiry; gardening as participatory, sensory practice — attention to affect, material relations, and temporal rhythms.
Extended fieldwork; participant observation; informal interviews; ecological walks with Cree hunters; ecological surveys of land and species.
Data richness / saturation
Extended fieldwork provides rich, multi-layered data; saturation implied through prolonged engagement with community practices and ecological cycles.
Data Analysis
Analytical approach
Interpretive; posthumanist conceptual analysis; develops theoretical framework from garden practices; garden used as vignette to think through organisational ethics.
Grounded theory–influenced; inductive thematic analysis; comparison between ecologically embedded (Cree) and non-embedded (conventional) managerial practices.
Reflexivity / positionality
Central to method — researchers participate in ecological relations rather than observing from outside; posthumanist reflexivity challenges researcher–researched divide.
Researcher positionality acknowledged; outsider relationship to Indigenous Cree community addressed; limitations of external researcher perspective noted.
Data linked to interpretations
Quality (Lincoln & Guba)
Credibility
Grounded in posthumanist scholarship; rich embodied engagement; peer-reviewed in JBE. No traditional member checking; vignette approach limits conventional credibility markers.
Strong — prolonged engagement with Cree community; participant observation + interviews = triangulation; rich descriptive data; published in top peer-reviewed journal (AMJ).
Transferability
Conceptual transferability high — garden as broadly applicable metaphor for organisational ecosystems. Limited empirical thick description of specific contexts limits traditional transferability.
Rich thick description of Cree context, land, and seasonal practices. Authors acknowledge limits for Western corporate settings — specificity of Indigenous context constrains direct transferability.
Dependability
Thinking-being methodology is philosophically transparent but less conventionally auditable than fieldwork-based research. Process of inquiry is described but not step-by-step reproducible.
Strong — detailed ethnographic fieldwork process documented; methodology section provides clear audit trail; extended time in field supports process consistency.
Confirmability
Interpretations grounded in posthumanist literature and embodied garden practice. Reflexivity present throughout; human-as-reference-point explicitly challenged as potential bias.
Interpretations grounded in Indigenous ecological knowledge and observed Cree practices. Reflexivity present regarding outsider-researcher position; data-interpretation links clear.
Other
Limitations acknowledged
Context-specific to Cree subarctic community; direct transferability to corporate settings limited. Published in 2000 — some concepts have developed since.
Contribution to knowledge
Advances relational posthuman ethics in business ethics; offers ecocentric organisational framework beyond anthropocentrism; contributes to more-than-human organising literature.
Introduced ecological embeddedness as a management concept; challenged decontextualised corporate sustainability; demonstrated value of Indigenous ecological knowledge for organisations.
